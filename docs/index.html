Film money
a data story
Films cost money.
However, there’s usually the hope of making that money back several times over. Often demanding huge amounts of effort, manpower and skill, films hazard a wager on human taste, and the resulting success or failure is sometimes spectacular.
Based on film budget, revenue and rating data this visual tells the story of the fickle relationship between business interests and public taste. It looks at the money films are made of and the money they make – focussing on the ends of the success spectrum; the extreme ups and downs (or rather lefts and rights); the winners, the losers and – probably most entertaining – the surprises. The biggest budgets
First let’s look at the big fish.
Up there are the 20 films with the biggest production budgets ever recorded.
Avatar is already a mature guest in this list being released in 2009. But it’s earned its top spot with a whopping $425 million budget, which translates to $470 million in 2016 dollars. However, Cleopatra is the real silverback in this band of films, released in ancient 1963. The film cost $42 million at the time which is the equivalent of a mere $330 million in 2016. An interesting feature for us to watch will be John Carter (previously known as John Carter of Mars) checking in at $280 million as the 9th biggest production so far.
In general you might notice that all films are what one would call Action or its close friend Adventure — apart from Cleopatra, Titanic and Tangled, the only Animation that cost the indecent sum of $300 million in 2016 money.

First from North American screenings
including United States, Canada, Puerto Rico and Guam. For brevity I shall call these revenues 'US revenues' - apologies, rather rude but much shorter

Keeping the production budget on screen in blue, we can already see how some films lost all worry-lines after just counting the US revenue. Avatar, Star Wars, Titanic and some others could’ve just packed up to lay back and stay at home, while others were surely hoping for more international dollars.
Let's sort by US revenue.

Titanic and Star Wars are in a good position having raked in more than double their costs just in the US. Avatar didn't quite get that far at home. But much more importantly our animated friend Tangled didn’t break even in the US while John Carter also can't have been very pleased with its US performance.
Let's now lift our eyes up to see what James Cameron has been spending his time on in the 90's and the early 2000's and enjoy the ride to the worldwide box-office revenues (including US - so all money made).
Overall Avatar made a mind boggling $3.1 billion only beaten by Titanic with $3.3 billion in overall revenue. Stacking the Avatar dollars would lift you up 339.3 km (211 miles) above sea-level (it would apparently). That would get you just 700 metres short of the International Space Station 340 km away. You might want to use a fire extinguisher for the last metres.Titanic gets you 20 km passed the ISS.
In other news (while sorting by worldwide revenue) we’re happy Tangled as our lonesome premium animation made it comfortably to the safe zone, while it seems John Carter actually just got its money worth. However — the production budget doesn’t show the marketing cost, which is pretty much exactly what it lost: around $150-200 million. Hurty. Wouldn’t quite make it to Mars.
